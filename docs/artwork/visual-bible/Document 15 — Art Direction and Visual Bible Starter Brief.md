# THE GARDEN STALL
## Document 15 — Art Direction and Visual Bible Starter Brief

**Working Title:** The Garden Stall  
**Document Type:** Art Direction / Visual Bible Starter / Graphics Team Brief  
**Status:** Draft 001  
**Purpose:** Define the initial visual identity, scene approach, camera, location mood, asset hierarchy, UI direction, animation limits, character/customer presentation, plant art treatment, and boundaries for The Garden Stall’s visual development.

---

# 1. Purpose of This Document

This document defines the starting visual direction for The Garden Stall.

It is not a complete production art bible.

It is the first accepted visual brief for the future Graphics / Artist window.

The goal is to give the project a clear art spine before individual mockups, prompts, UI experiments, or production assets are created.

The visual direction should support the game’s core identity:

> A rough little mobile plant stall gradually becoming more beautiful, skilled, and loved, set against richly drawn British urban and village scenes.

The visual style should create charm without making the starting business feel polished too early.

The player should begin with a rough setup and earn beauty over time.

---

# 2. Core Visual Principle

The Garden Stall’s beauty should come first from the world.

The backgrounds should be rich, atmospheric, and hand-drawn.

The stall begins rough.

The plants bring colour.

The player’s effort brings order.

Upgrades gradually bring charm.

Visual progression should be part of the emotional reward.

The starting stall should not look like a finished cosy boutique. It should look like:

> A practical, slightly battered little plant-selling setup that might become something wonderful.

---

# 3. Overall Style Target

The target visual mood is:

- hand-drawn
- adult storybook
- warm but grounded
- charming but not childish
- gently cinematic
- painterly enough to feel crafted
- readable enough for systems play
- rooted in real UK plant retail
- lightly inspired by Studio Ghibli atmosphere without copying any specific film, character, or asset

Useful descriptive phrase:

> Adult storybook warmth with grounded UK market-stall roughness.

The world should feel lived-in, local, and slightly imperfect.

---

# 4. Visual Hierarchy

The game should use different detail levels for different visual layers.

## 4.1 Highest Detail: Static Backgrounds

Location backgrounds should be the most beautiful and richly drawn elements.

They are mostly static and can therefore carry the visual charm of the game.

Backgrounds should include:

- atmosphere
- light
- weather
- architecture
- street/village detail
- depth
- small environmental storytelling
- visual identity for each location

## 4.2 Medium Detail: Stall and Movable Objects

The van, tables, trays, furniture, decorations, and display structures should be simpler than backgrounds but still charming.

They must remain readable as gameplay pieces.

They should support:

- moving stock
- tray placement
- display zones
- upgrade recognition
- furniture comparison
- condition feedback

## 4.3 Lower Detail: In-Scene Customers

Customers in the live scene are not detailed character illustrations.

They should appear like passer-by silhouettes or lightly animated pavement figures.

They may approach, browse, and leave.

Their detailed identity is shown on their customer card, not through full scene animation.

## 4.4 Separate High Detail: Hero Shots

Plants, customers, locations, and key discoveries may receive detailed hero illustrations elsewhere.

Examples:

- plant hero shots
- customer cards
- location cards
- notebook discoveries
- weekly report highlights
- supplier feature cards

This lets the game use beautiful art without overcomplicating the live stall view.

---

# 5. Camera and Scene View

The main stall scene should use:

> Slight isometric / three-quarter view.

Purpose:

- show table surface
- show trays
- show floor space
- show van
- show customers approaching
- show location background
- support display management
- preserve charm and spatial clarity

The scene should feel like a small diorama set into a richly painted location.

Alternative views may be explored later, such as:

- side-on pavement view
- closer display view
- customer card view
- notebook view
- wholesaler browser view
- location overview/map view

Prototype 0.1 should use one primary three-quarter scene view.

---

# 6. Background Philosophy

Backgrounds are static and richly drawn.

This is where the game should invest visual beauty early.

Each location background should feel like a place.

The background should not be a generic backdrop. It should communicate:

- local identity
- time of day
- likely customer type
- trading mood
- weather feel
- economic opportunity
- pace
- charm

Because backgrounds are static, they can be more detailed than gameplay objects.

They should be suitable for limited ambient animation overlays.

# 9. Vehicle Direction

The starting vehicle should be:

> an old small van with faded colour.

It should feel rough and practical, but more characterful than a plain white Transit.

It should not be pretty yet.

Possible traits:

- faded paint
- small dents
- worn bumper
- mismatched panel or touch-up patch
- basic open rear doors
- slightly improvised storage
- no polished branding
- cheap trader feel

The vehicle should be part of the stall identity.

It should visually improve through upgrades and branching vehicle choices.

---

# 10. Vehicle Upgrade Paths

The first major vehicle upgrade should present two viable playstyle paths.

## 10.1 Charming Path

Example:

> Pretty burger-van-style trailer or small vintage sales trailer.

Characteristics:

- more charming
- more attractive
- better display identity
- smaller space upgrade
- good for gift, village, market, and boutique-style play
- less efficient for bulk high-turnover logistics

This path supports players who want beauty, customer appeal, and curated display.

## 10.2 Logistics Path

Example:

> Larger battered Transit-style van.

Characteristics:

- more storage
- more stock movement
- better high-turnover capacity
- rougher appearance
- less charming
- practical for bulk bedding, car-boot style, market volume, and scaling

This path supports players who want efficiency, stock capacity, and turnover.

Both paths should be viable.

The game should not imply that charm is always better than logistics, or logistics always better than charm.

---

# 11. Stall Starting Aesthetic

The starting stall should be properly rough.

Initial elements:

- old faded small van
- folding trestle table
- plastic nursery trays
- cheap handwritten labels
- floor stacks
- basic reduced basket
- no tablecloth
- no polished signage
- no nice crates
- no awning
- no beautiful shelves
- no coherent branding

The scene should still have charm because:

- the plants are colourful
- the background is beautiful
- the player arranges the display
- the lighting is warm
- the world feels human

But the stall itself begins rough.

The player earns charm.

---

# 12. Static Gameplay Objects

Gameplay objects should be static but movable.

Examples:

- vehicle
- trestle table
- trays
- floor stacks
- reduced basket
- shelving
- crates
- pots
- compost
- decorative objects
- awning
- signs
- furniture upgrades

These should be visually simpler than backgrounds.

They need:

- clear silhouettes
- readable condition states
- consistent scale
- easy recognition
- compatibility with multiple backgrounds
- ability to be repositioned or swapped
- charm without excessive detail

They should not visually fight the background.

---

# 13. Plants in Stall View

Plants in the live stall view can be simple.

They must communicate:

- plant type/category
- colour
- size
- tray fullness
- rough condition
- flowering/greenery distinction
- floor/table suitability
- reduced/tired state

They do not need full botanical detail in the live stall view.

A tray may be represented by:

- grouped colour blobs/shapes
- simple leaves/flowers
- fullness level
- condition tint/pose
- label/icon overlay

Important live-view plant readability:

- full tray vs gappy tray
- watered vs dry warning
- fresh vs tired
- flower colour group
- tall/floor pot vs tray stock
- sundry vs plant

The live view is functional and charming, not botanical illustration.

---

Plants should also have detailed hero shots for other uses.

Hero shots may appear in:

- plant pages

Hero shot style:

---

# 15. Customer Scene Presentation

Customers in the live scene should appear as passers-by.

They are not detailed animated character sprites.

In-scene customers should be:

- silhouettes or semi-simplified figures
- lightly animated
- pavement-scale
- readable by movement rather than facial detail
- part of the scene flow
- able to approach, pause, browse, and leave

They should feel like people passing on a street, market, or village green.

They should not require full character animation.

---

# 16. Customer Cards

Detailed customer inspection happens through customer cards.

Clicking or selecting a customer opens their card.

Customer cards may show:

- name
- archetype
- small portrait or silhouette thumbnail
- request text
- known quirks
- previous purchases
- satisfaction history
- follow-up options
- budget clues
- notebook memory
- recommendation result

Style:

> notebook/customer memory page, with small portrait treatment later.

Customer card visuals should be more detailed than in-scene passer-by silhouettes.

They can carry personality without requiring complex scene sprites.

---

# 17. Animation Direction

Prototype animation should be minimal and ambient.

Required feel:

> limited motion that makes static scenes feel alive.

Examples:

- drip from an awning
- buzzing bee
- flickering neon
- subtle passing silhouettes
- slight plant sway
- small sparkle of rain
- steam in night market
- blinking station sign
- drifting leaf
- page/card slide transitions
- customer approach/browse/leave loops

Avoid heavy animation requirements.

Prototype 0.1 should not require:

- full character walk cycles
- detailed facial animation
- full plant growth animation
- complex weather simulation
- fully animated traffic
- bespoke animation for every customer

Motion should be sparse, atmospheric, and useful.

# 18. User Interface Direction

Prototype 0.1 UI should lean toward clean game UI.

As the visual identity matures, the UI can move gradually toward a hybrid of clean UI and diegetic notebook/cards/labels.

## 18.1 Prototype 0.1 UI

Priorities:

- readability
- fast testing
- clear economy
- clear stock state
- clear menus
- easy debug controls
- visible tray/condition data
- usable weekly report tabs

Prototype UI can use simple panels, cards, and tables.

## 18.2 Future UI Direction

Longer-term UI should become more styled.

Possible visual motifs:

- notebook pages
- trader ledger
- plant labels
- price labels
- supplier website cards
- handwritten annotations
- customer memory cards
- weekly report tabs
- clipboard/checklist
- small stickers/badges for discoveries

Target:

> Clean structure with increasing handmade charm.

Do not sacrifice clarity for decoration.

# 21. Weekly Report Visual Direction

# 32. Visual Acceptance Criteria

A visual mockup is successful if it:

- feels hand-drawn
- feels adult rather than childish
- supports gameplay readability
- preserves rough starting progression
- makes the location feel distinct
- lets plants remain readable
- does not over-polish the starting stall
- has charm without becoming twee
- feels grounded in UK plant retail
- can be repeated consistently
- can become a practical game asset direction

Reject or revise if it:

- looks like generic mobile game art
- looks too glossy
- looks too childish
- looks too fantasy
- looks too much like a finished boutique
- hides gameplay information
- uses backgrounds that overpower the stall
- makes customers too detailed in live scene
- loses the rough-to-beautiful progression

# 35. Final Visual Direction Definition

The Garden Stall should look like a hand-drawn, adult storybook game about a rough mobile plant stall slowly becoming charming through skill, care, and earned upgrades.

Static backgrounds carry much of the beauty: richly drawn village greens, urban station corners, market squares, and later neon night markets. The playable stall objects are simpler and clearer, but still warm. Customers move through the scene as passer-by silhouettes, with detail reserved for cards and notebook memory. Plants are simplified in the live display but receive detailed hero shots elsewhere. Animation is limited and atmospheric. The starting stall is genuinely rough, and charm is something the player builds.

The core visual question is:

> “Does this scene make me want to set up my rough little stall here and make it beautiful?”
