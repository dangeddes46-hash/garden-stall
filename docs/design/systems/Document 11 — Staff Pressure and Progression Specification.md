# THE GARDEN STALL
## Document 11 — Staff Pressure and Progression Specification

**Working Title:** The Garden Stall  
**Document Type:** Staff System / Workload Pressure / Progression Specification  
**Status:** Draft 001  
**Purpose:** Define why staff become necessary, when they unlock, what work they relieve, how staff learn, how wages and morale work, why staff may leave, and how the first prototype should hint at staff pressure without allowing active hiring.

---

# 1. Purpose of This Document

This document defines the staff pressure and progression system for The Garden Stall.

Staff are not part of Prototype 0.1 active play.

However, the game should begin creating the feeling that staff will eventually become necessary.

The player starts alone because the opening business is too small and too cash-tight to justify wages. By the end of the first prototype week, the player should begin to feel the shape of future pressure:

- more stock than one person can manage comfortably
- trays becoming gappy during trading
- watering and tidying competing with customer requests
- van restocking taking valuable time
- higher-footfall locations being profitable but demanding
- larger displays becoming hard to keep full alone

Staff should not exist as a separate HR minigame.

Staff exist to relieve business pressure and allow the stall to grow.

The core staff question is:

> “What work is now valuable enough that I should pay someone else to help with it?”

---

# 2. Core Principle

Staff are a progression response to workload.

The player should first understand the solo loop:

- ordering
- collecting
- loading the van
- choosing location
- setting up display
- pricing
- selling
- watering
- tidying
- consolidating
- reducing
- restocking
- handling requests
- ordering again

Only after that loop becomes busy should staff enter.

Staff should make the player feel:

> “I know exactly why I need help.”

Not:

> “The game has randomly unlocked another management system.”

---

# 3. Prototype 0.1 Staff Rule

Prototype 0.1 should not allow active staff hiring.

Staff are excluded from active play.

However, Prototype 0.1 should include staff pressure signals.

## 3.1 Prototype Staff Signals

By Day 5–7, reports may say:

> The stall was hard to keep tidy during the busy period.

> You missed a chance to restock the front table while handling a customer request.

> A helper could water trays while you deal with special requests.

> Larger displays will be difficult to manage alone.

> You handled the week, but a busier pitch would stretch one pair of hands.

## 3.2 Prototype Goal

The player should finish Prototype 0.1 thinking:

> “I do not need staff yet, but I can see why I will.”

---

# 4. When Staff Become Available

Staff should become available after the player demonstrates business viability.

They should not be available immediately.

Possible unlock requirements:

- complete first 7-day prototype week
- reach a minimum reputation level
- achieve a strong sales day
- unlock a larger display upgrade
- trade at a busier location
- hit a weekly turnover threshold
- reach a point where workload pressure is visible

Recommended first unlock:

> Staff become visible as a future option after the first successful week, but are not economically sensible until the player has either upgraded display space or reached stronger turnover.

This prevents hiring too early from being the obvious answer.

---

# 5. Why Staff Should Not Be Economically Valid Too Early

Early in the game, staff should be too expensive relative to turnover.

The player begins with:

- small stock volume
- rough table
- limited display
- low cash
- small daily profit
- incomplete reputation
- no stable high-footfall pitch

Hiring at this stage would often be a mistake.

This is important.

The game should teach that staff are not free automation. They are a business cost that only makes sense when the work they enable creates more value than their wage.

The player should learn:

> “Hiring someone before I can keep them busy is a waste.”

---

# 6. Staff Availability Stages

Staff progression can use stages.

## 6.1 Stage 0 — Solo Trader

Prototype 0.1.

No staff.

Player does all work.

Pressure is felt but manageable.

## 6.2 Stage 1 — Casual Helper

First staff stage.

A helper may work:

- one day
- a weekend
- short shift
- busy location only
- cash-in-hand style abstraction, if legally neutralised in final writing

Gameplay role:

- reduces one type of labour
- cheap but limited
- low skill
- unreliable or unavailable some days

## 6.3 Stage 2 — Part-Time Assistant

More reliable staff.

Works scheduled shifts.

Can develop skills.

Wage matters.

May leave if underpaid.

## 6.4 Stage 3 — Experienced Assistant

More expensive but meaningful.

Can handle a domain independently.

May improve sales, display, or care quality.

## 6.5 Stage 4 — Specialist Staff

Later game.

Examples:

- plant expert
- display merchandiser
- sales assistant
- nursery/back-of-house helper
- supplier/order assistant
- event helper

Specialists should appear only once the business has grown.

---

# 7. Staff Roles

Staff should be defined by what work they help with.

## 7.1 Care Helper

Focus:

- watering
- spotting dry trays
- preventing condition decline
- maintaining thirsty stock
- basic deadheading later

Best for:

- bedding-heavy displays
- herbs
- hot weather
- large stock volume
- fragile stock

Weakness:

- may not improve sales directly
- may not handle special requests well

## 7.2 Display Helper

Focus:

- tidying trays
- consolidating partial trays
- filling front table gaps
- keeping reduced area contained
- improving display fullness

Best for:

- busy trading days
- large tray displays
- high-footfall locations
- furniture-heavy setups

Weakness:

- may not water intelligently unless trained
- may not advise customers

## 7.3 Sales Helper

Focus:

- basic customer handling
- till work
- upselling simple add-ons
- handling bargain/gift customers
- reducing missed sales while player does display work

Best for:

- high footfall
- Station Corner
- Market Square rushes
- gift-heavy stock
- simple stock range

Weakness:

- may make poor recommendations if plant knowledge is low

## 7.4 Plant Knowledge Helper

Focus:

- special requests
- plant suitability
- care advice
- safety warnings
- customer trust
- reducing bad recommendations

Best for:

- specialist stock
- expensive plants
- unusual customer requests
- reputation building

Weakness:

- more expensive
- may not be fast at manual display labour

## 7.5 Back-of-House Helper

Focus:

- home stock watering
- unloading/loading
- stock checks
- order preparation
- basic nursery/facility work later

Best for:

- larger home stock
- greenhouse/nursery upgrades
- multiple suppliers
- propagation systems, if added later

Weakness:

- less visible at the stall
- helps tomorrow more than today

---

# 8. Staff Task System

Staff should perform assigned tasks rather than act as vague passive bonuses.

The player assigns a staff member to one or more duties for the day.

Prototype future task examples:

- Water visible trays
- Keep front table full
- Consolidate gappy trays
- Manage reduced section
- Serve basic customers
- Watch till
- Upsell compost/pots
- Handle gift buyers
- Help with special requests
- Load/unload van
- Check home stock
- Prepare tomorrow’s order

Each task should connect to existing systems.

This keeps staff grounded in the labour the player already understands.

---

# 9. Staff Actions During Trading

During trading, staff may act automatically based on their assignment.

Examples:

## Watering Assignment

The helper checks moisture and waters trays that are Dry or near Dry.

Higher skill means:

- better timing
- less overwatering
- faster work
- fewer missed trays

## Display Assignment

The helper tidies or consolidates gappy trays.

Higher skill means:

- better prioritisation
- better mixed tray choices
- less loss of attractiveness
- faster restocking

## Sales Assignment

The helper serves simple customers.

Higher skill means:

- faster service
- better add-on sales
- fewer missed customers
- better price confidence

## Request Assignment

The helper can suggest answers or handle low-complexity requests.

Higher skill means:

- better recommendations
- safer advice
- fewer unsuitable sales

Staff should not make the player obsolete.

They create room for the player to focus on higher-value decisions.

---

# 10. Staff Skill Areas

Staff can level up in specific areas.

Recommended skill areas:

1. Care
2. Display
3. Sales
4. Plant Knowledge
5. Reliability
6. Speed

## 10.1 Care

Improves:

- watering judgement
- deadheading
- moisture recovery
- prevention of dry/bone-dry damage
- avoiding waterlogging

## 10.2 Display

Improves:

- tidying
- consolidation
- display fullness
- colour/theme awareness
- reduced area management
- furniture use later

## 10.3 Sales

Improves:

- customer throughput
- add-on sales
- price confidence
- bargain/gift handling
- basic upselling

## 10.4 Plant Knowledge

Improves:

- special request handling
- plant suitability advice
- customer trust
- safety warnings
- location/plant fit suggestions

## 10.5 Reliability

Improves:

- showing up
- sticking to tasks
- fewer mistakes
- better performance under pressure
- lower chance of quitting abruptly

## 10.6 Speed

Improves:

- time cost of tasks
- restocking speed
- watering speed
- till/customer speed
- setup/pack-down assistance

---

# 11. Staff Learning

Staff learn by doing.

A staff member gains experience in the area they are assigned to.

Examples:

- watering all day gives Care XP
- tidying/consolidating gives Display XP
- serving customers gives Sales XP
- helping with requests gives Plant Knowledge XP
- repeatedly completing shifts builds Reliability
- high-pressure trading can build Speed

Learning should feel organic.

A staff member who spends ten days watering trays should become better at watering trays, not randomly better at sales.

---

# 12. Staff Level-Up Effects

Level-ups should produce understandable benefits.

Examples:

## Care Level 2

> Notices Dry trays earlier.

## Care Level 3

> Less likely to overwater while rushing.

## Display Level 2

> Tidies gappy trays more efficiently.

## Display Level 3

> Can identify simple strong pairings when consolidating.

## Sales Level 2

> More likely to add compost or pots to grow-your-own sales.

## Plant Knowledge Level 2

> Handles basic beginner questions without player intervention.

## Reliability Level 2

> Less likely to miss assigned tasks during rush periods.

These should be readable benefits, not hidden stat bumps only.

---

# 13. Staff Mistakes

Staff should sometimes make mistakes, especially when inexperienced or underpaid.

Mistakes should be light and understandable.

Examples:

## Care Mistakes

- waters too late
- misses a dry tray
- overwaters a sensitive plant
- waters already damp trays, risking waterlogging

## Display Mistakes

- leaves a gappy tray too long
- consolidates into an unattractive mixed tray
- puts reduced stock too prominently
- fails to restock a front table gap

## Sales Mistakes

- fails to upsell compost/pots
- sells at wrong price band if poorly trained, later
- misses a customer during rush
- recommends a poor fit on a simple request

## Knowledge Mistakes

- fails to warn about a plant suitability issue
- gives generic advice
- cannot identify a customer’s described plant
- suggests a plant that is acceptable but not ideal

Mistakes should create texture, not rage.

The player should usually understand why the mistake happened.

---

# 14. Staff Wages

Staff cost money.

Wage must matter.

A staff member should not be automatically profitable.

## 14.1 Wage Categories

Possible wage tiers:

- Casual helper: low daily wage
- Part-time assistant: medium daily wage
- Experienced assistant: higher wage
- Specialist: high wage

Prototype later values might be:

| Staff Type | Example Daily Wage |
|---|---:|
| Casual Helper | £25–£40 |
| Part-Time Assistant | £40–£60 |
| Experienced Assistant | £60–£90 |
| Specialist | £80–£120+ |

These values are placeholders.

They should be tuned against expected daily profit.

## 14.2 Wage Philosophy

A helper is worth hiring only if they allow the player to earn more than they cost.

Examples:

- they prevent £30 of stock loss
- they enable £80 more sales at a busy pitch
- they keep the display full enough to convert more customers
- they allow the player to handle profitable special requests
- they support larger stock volume

This makes staff an economic decision.

---

# 15. Wage Satisfaction

Staff should notice when they are underpaid for their level.

As staff improve, they may expect better pay.

This creates a progression pressure.

A trained helper is valuable, but more expensive to keep.

## 15.1 Wage Satisfaction States

Suggested states:

- Happy
- Fair
- Underpaid
- Unhappy
- Likely to Leave

## 15.2 Underpaying Staff

If staff are underpaid, effects may include:

- morale decline
- slower XP gain
- lower reliability
- higher mistake chance
- refusal of extra shifts
- eventual leaving

Underpaying should not cause instant punishment.

It should create warning signs.

---

# 16. Staff Leaving

Staff may leave if:

- consistently underpaid
- overworked
- ignored after levelling up
- asked to do work they hate
- business reputation is poor
- better opportunity appears
- low morale persists

Staff leaving should not be random.

It should be understandable and foreshadowed.

Example warning:

> Maya has become much better at display work and feels her current pay no longer reflects her value.

Later:

> Maya is considering other work unless her wage improves.

Final:

> Maya has left for a more reliable garden centre job.

This makes staff feel like people without turning the game into a soap opera.

---

# 17. Staff Personality

Staff may eventually have light personality traits.

Keep this modest.

Traits should affect work style.

Examples:

## Practical

Better at watering, loading, physical work.

## Chatty

Good with customers, slower at repetitive tasks.

## Tidy

Good display helper, dislikes rough reduced sections.

## Plant Nerd

Good knowledge, may be slower at sales.

## Fast Worker

Good speed, more mistakes if untrained.

## Reliable

Fewer absences, slower skill growth perhaps.

Do not overload the system early.

Personality should add flavour and small mechanical differences.

---

# 18. Staff Preference

Staff may prefer certain work.

Examples:

- likes care tasks
- likes customer work
- dislikes heavy lifting
- enjoys display
- wants to learn plants
- dislikes chaotic rushes
- enjoys market days

If assigned to preferred work:

- morale improves
- XP gain may improve
- fewer mistakes

If repeatedly assigned to disliked work:

- morale declines
- mistakes may increase
- they may eventually leave

This should be a later layer, not required for first staff implementation.

---

# 19. Staff and Player Skill Tree

Staff should interact with the player’s progression.

Examples:

- player skill unlocks better staff instructions
- staff skill unlocks automation
- staff can discover display pairings
- experienced staff contribute notebook/codex entries
- player can train staff in unlocked knowledge
- staff can specialise based on repeated assignments

The player’s skill tree should primarily improve judgement and information.

Staff progression should primarily improve execution.

Together:

> The player learns what to do. Staff help get it done.

---

# 20. Staff and Special Requests

Staff may help with special requests in several ways.

## 20.1 Basic Stage

Staff can keep the stall running while the player handles a request.

This is the most important early value.

Example:

> While you advised a customer on tomatoes, Maya kept watering the front trays.

## 20.2 Intermediate Stage

Staff can suggest possible matches.

Example:

> Maya thinks the strawberry plant plus a terracotta pot fits this request.

## 20.3 Advanced Stage

Experienced staff can handle simple requests automatically.

Example:

> Tom handled a beginner herb request and sold basil, parsley, and a pot.

## 20.4 Risk

Low-knowledge staff may make weak recommendations.

The player may choose whether staff can handle requests independently.

---

# 21. Staff and Display Pairings

Display-skilled staff may notice or preserve pairings.

Examples:

- keeps tomato and basil near each other
- places petunia and lobelia together
- avoids chaotic mixed trays
- tidies trays without breaking a useful display theme
- suggests pairing stock with pots/compost

At higher levels, a staff member may contribute to notebook unlocks.

Example:

> Maya noticed customers kept buying petunias with lobelia. Notebook updated.

This makes staff feel helpful beyond raw labour.

---

# 22. Staff and Reduced Stock

Staff can help manage reduced stock.

Tasks:

- identify tired plants
- move stock to reduced section
- keep reduced section tidy
- prevent reduced stock dominating main display
- suggest end-of-day reductions
- clear unsellable stock

A weak staff member may over-reduce or leave tired stock too long.

A strong staff member can improve stock recovery and reduce wastage.

---

# 23. Staff and Home Stock

Later, staff can help with home stock.

Tasks:

- water stock at home
- check moisture
- sort old stock
- prepare tomorrow’s van loadout
- unload the van
- organise trays
- maintain greenhouse/cold frame/propagator
- flag stock that should be reduced tomorrow

This becomes more important when home storage upgrades arrive.

Prototype 0.1 should not include active home-staff systems.

---

# 24. Staff and Vehicle/Setup

Staff can reduce setup and pack-down pressure.

Tasks:

- load van faster
- unload stock
- set up tables
- place trays
- pack away at end
- reduce travel/setup time penalties
- help larger displays become viable

This connects staff to future larger stall layouts.

The first big reason to hire may be:

> “I have more stock and display space than I can set up and maintain alone.”

---

# 25. Staff Scheduling

Early staff scheduling should be simple.

Possible model:

- choose staff for the day
- assign primary task
- assign secondary task if skilled enough
- pay daily wage
- staff works that trading day

Avoid complex rota management early.

Later systems may add:

- weekly availability
- part-time days
- events
- sickness
- preferred days
- staff fatigue
- multiple shifts

Prototype first staff version should use daily hiring/assignment.

---

# 26. Staff UI

Staff screen should show:

- name
- wage
- morale
- skill levels
- preferred tasks
- current assignment
- expected benefit
- warning if underpaid
- warning if workload is too high
- notes from recent shifts

Example card:

**Maya**

- Wage: £45/day
- Morale: Fair
- Skills: Care 2, Display 1, Sales 0, Knowledge 0
- Prefers: Care, tidy work
- Assignment: Water and tidy front table
- Note: “She has become quicker at spotting dry trays.”

---

# 27. Staff Daily Report

End-of-day report should include a staff section when staff exist.

Fields:

- tasks completed
- missed tasks
- mistakes
- sales supported
- stock saved
- XP gained
- morale change
- wage satisfaction
- notable moments

Example:

> Maya watered 14 trays, prevented two busy lizzie trays from drying out, and gained Care XP. She missed one gappy petunia tray during the afternoon rush.

This helps the player understand whether staff were worth paying.

---

# 28. Staff Value Reporting

The game should help the player see staff value without exact over-analysis.

Possible feedback:

- “Maya prevented several trays drying out.”
- “Tom’s till help reduced missed customers during the busy period.”
- “Display stayed fuller than usual.”
- “Wage cost was high relative to today’s sales.”
- “A quieter pitch may not need staff yet.”

The player should learn:

> Staff are profitable when matched to the right pressure.

# 33. Staff Pressure Metrics

The game can track hidden pressure metrics.

Examples:

- unwatered tray events
- gappy tray time
- missed restock opportunities
- missed customers
- abandoned special requests
- overloaded display actions
- unsold stock due to poor visibility
- stock lost due to lack of care
- setup/pack-down time pressure

These metrics can trigger staff hints.

Example:

> You spent most of the afternoon maintaining trays. A display helper could free you to serve more customers.

This makes staff recommendations evidence-based.

---

# 34. Hiring Too Early

The game should warn if hiring appears economically risky.

Example:

> Today’s expected footfall may not justify a helper’s wage.

Or:

> With your current display size, you may not have enough work to keep paid help profitable.

The player may still hire if allowed.

But the game should make the risk clear.

---

# 35. Staff and Difficulty

Staff can support different difficulty modes.

## Relaxed Mode

- staff make fewer mistakes
- wage pressure lower
- morale declines slower
- clearer task recommendations

## Standard Mode

- balanced costs and mistakes
- underpaying matters
- staff learn normally

## Expert Mode

- staff wages tighter
- mistakes more consequential
- morale and task fit matter more
- less hand-holding on staff value

Prototype does not need difficulty modes, but the staff system should support them later.

# 36. Prototype 0.1 Staff Hints

Prototype 0.1 should include only hints, not active staff.

Suggested Day 7 weekly report section:

> **Workload Note**  
> You managed the stall alone this week, but busy moments stretched the day. Watering, tray consolidation, and customer requests competed for your time. A helper would not be affordable yet, but a larger display or busier pitch may soon make help worthwhile.

If the player had many dry trays:

> A care helper could have prevented some watering pressure.

If the player had many gappy trays:

> A display helper could have kept the table fuller.

If the player missed customers:

> A sales helper could have reduced lost browsing sales.

If the player failed requests due to time:

> Help at the stall would let you spend more time on customer advice.

This previews the future system.

# 37. First Staff Implementation Scope

When staff are first implemented after Prototype 0.1, keep scope narrow.

Recommended first implementation:

- 2–3 staff candidates
- daily wage
- one assignment per day
- 3 skill areas only: Care, Display, Sales
- simple XP gain
- morale state
- underpaid warning
- end-of-day staff report
- no complex scheduling
- no staff leaving immediately
- no home stock work yet
- no specialist knowledge staff until later

This allows the system to prove value without becoming too large.

# 39. Staff Success Criteria

The staff system succeeds if:

- the player understands why staff are useful
- staff relieve real pressures the player has already felt
- hiring too early can be a mistake
- staff feel like people but not a separate life sim
- staff learning feels connected to assigned work
- wages matter
- underpaying creates understandable risk
- staff leaving is foreshadowed
- different staff solve different problems
- staff support business growth rather than replacing the player

The system fails if:

- staff are just passive stat boosts
- staff are mandatory before the player understands the loop
- staff are always profitable
- staff are never profitable
- staff mistakes feel random or unfair
- wage/morale systems dominate the game
- staff management becomes more important than stock, display, and selling

# 41. Final Staff Definition

Staff in The Garden Stall are not an early automation feature. They are a business progression response to workload.

The player begins alone and learns the value of labour by experiencing the pressure of watering, tidying, consolidating, restocking, serving customers, and handling requests. Staff become useful only when the business has enough stock, display space, footfall, or specialist demand to justify wages. They learn by doing, expect fair pay as they improve, and may leave if consistently underpaid or misused.

The core staff question is:

> “Which part of this growing stall is now too valuable for me to keep doing alone?”
