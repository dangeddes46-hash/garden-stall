# THE GARDEN STALL

## Document 1 — Core Vision Brief

**Working Title:** The Garden Stall
**Document Type:** Core Vision / Project Identity
**Status:** Draft 001
**Purpose:** Define the central identity, tone, boundaries, and design pillars of The Garden Stall before mechanical and content specifications are developed.

---

# 1. Vision Statement

**The Garden Stall** is a cosy mobile plant retail sim about buying seasonal wholesale stock, arranging attractive displays, setting prices, and matching living plants to the people and places where they will thrive.

The player begins with an ugly transit-style van, a basic trestle table, and a modest amount of money. Each day, they choose where to trade, decide what plants to stock, arrange the stall, and sell to customers whose needs are shaped by location, season, budget, taste, and gardening experience.

The game is about the small satisfactions of retail judgement:

* choosing the right stock
* making the stall look inviting
* spotting what customers are likely to want
* pricing fairly or cleverly
* clearing tired stock before it becomes worthless
* helping people find plants that suit their homes, gardens, and lives

The player is not running a large garden centre. They are building a small mobile plant business into something loved, recognisable, and locally trusted.

The core fantasy is:

> “I know my stock, I know my customers, and I can turn this scruffy little van into a beautiful travelling garden stall.”

---

# 2. High Concept

**Tiny Bookshop, but for plants — with living stock, seasonal demand, visual display, and light care pressure.**

The Garden Stall shares a broad cosy retail structure with games like Tiny Bookshop:

* choose a trading location
* curate stock
* sell to customers
* handle occasional recommendation challenges
* improve and decorate the shop
* grow reputation over time

Its distinct identity comes from the fact that plants are alive.

Plants can:

* wilt
* pass their flowering peak
* look tired
* clash or harmonise in displays
* sell better in certain seasons
* suit some customers but not others
* need light care
* become reduced stock
* die if neglected for too long

This creates a different kind of selling puzzle. The player is not merely asking, “What will sell?” They are asking:

> “What will sell here, today, to these people, while it still looks good?”

---

# 3. Emotional Target

The game should feel:

* cosy
* practical
* gently busy
* warm
* tactile
* seasonal
* lightly educational
* visually satisfying
* charming but grounded

The player should feel clever, not stressed.

The game should create the feeling of a good working day on a small stall: there is always something useful to do, but the pressure is mostly pleasant. Water this tray. Fill that gap. Move those past-peak bulbs to reduced. Put the lavender near the front. Help the customer who wants something for a shady bathroom. Check tomorrow’s market. Order more herbs.

The fantasy is not idleness. It is **pleasant competence**.

The stall should gradually become personal. At the start, it is temporary, ugly, and basic. Over time, the player should feel they have built something recognisable:

> “That’s my stall. I chose those crates, that awning, that colour scheme, that reduced basket, that sign, and those plants.”

---

# 4. Primary Design Pillars

## 4.1 Stock and Sell Comes First

The Garden Stall is primarily a **stock-and-sell game**.

The central decisions are:

* what to buy
* where to sell
* how to price
* how to display
* what to reduce
* what customers to satisfy
* when to expand capacity
* when to hire help

Plant care is present, but it is not the main game.

The player should spend more time thinking like a small plant retailer than like a grower, farmer, botanist, or greenhouse manager.

---

## 4.2 Living Stock Creates Gentle Pressure

Plants are not static inventory.

They are commercial stock with condition, seasonality, and presentation value.

A plant may be profitable one week and awkward the next. A flowering bulb may sell well while blooming, then become much harder to shift. A tray of herbs may be a safe buy at a farmers’ market but a poor fit at a station corner. A bargain batch from the wholesaler may look tempting but require quick sale before it declines.

Living stock should create decisions such as:

* Do I buy extra because this should sell well tomorrow?
* Do I risk a cheap batch that is already slightly tired?
* Do I reduce these now or hope they sell tomorrow?
* Do I keep these for a better location?
* Do I spend time tidying the display or serving customers?
* Do I need staff because I am carrying too much stock to maintain alone?

The pressure should be real, but not punishing.

---

## 4.3 Display Is a Sales System

Display is central to the game.

The stall is not a passive inventory list. It is a selling surface.

A good display should:

* draw customers in
* increase browsing sales
* improve perceived value
* support premium pricing
* make plants easier to discover
* create seasonal atmosphere
* make the stall feel cared for

Display quality may be affected by:

* empty tray slots
* colour harmony
* tidy arrangement
* plant condition
* furniture choice
* seasonal relevance
* height and shape variation
* location suitability
* reduced stock placement
* featured plants

The player should care about how the stall looks because it affects how well it sells.

Display should be meaningful but not fiddly. It should use clear zones, trays, shelves, crates, stands, and feature spaces rather than demanding precise freeform placement.

---

## 4.4 Location Shapes Demand

Where the player trades should meaningfully shape the day.

Each location has a customer profile, budget range, footfall level, display expectation, and plant demand pattern.

A good stock choice in one location may be mediocre in another.

Examples:

* A city station corner may favour compact houseplants, gifts, and fast purchases.
* A village green may favour bedding plants, cottage flowers, pollinator plants, and traditional gardeners.
* A farmers’ market may favour herbs, vegetable seedlings, wildlife plants, and practical buyers.
* A garden open day may favour experienced customers, specialist stock, and more difficult requests.

Location choice should be one of the main strategic decisions in the game.

---

## 4.5 Customers Have Needs, Not Just Wallets

Customers are not simply buyers with money. They have needs, tastes, constraints, assumptions, and levels of gardening knowledge.

Most customers browse passively. They buy if the stall contains plants that match their needs at a price they accept, displayed in a way that attracts them.

Some customers create special requests. These are direct recommendation challenges where the player actively helps.

Customer needs may include:

* “I have a shady garden.”
* “I need a gift.”
* “I always forget to water.”
* “I want bees.”
* “I rent and only have pots.”
* “I have a cat.”
* “I want colour right now.”
* “I remember a plant my neighbour used to grow.”

The best sale is not always the highest price. Sometimes the satisfying sale is recommending the plant that will actually work.

---

## 4.6 Special Requests Create Memorable Moments

Approximately one in ten customers should create a special request.

These are the game’s focused interaction moments.

They may ask the player to:

* identify a plant from a vague description
* choose a plant for awkward conditions
* assemble a companion pot
* suggest a gift
* recommend something pet-safe or child-safe
* redirect them away from an unsuitable choice
* find something they would not discover through passive browsing

Special requests should be rewarding because they let the player use judgement.

Success should improve:

* sale value
* customer satisfaction
* review score
* shop reputation
* word of mouth
* return customer chance
* location profile

These moments should make the stall feel human.

---

## 4.7 Staff Are a Natural Response to Growth

The player begins doing everything themselves.

As stock volume, display complexity, and customer demand grow, the player should feel the need for help.

Staff are not just abstract upgrades. They are people who learn, improve, and become valuable.

Staff may help with:

* watering
* tidying
* pruning/deadheading
* tray consolidation
* display maintenance
* passive sales
* customer patience
* special request support
* restocking
* reduced section management

Staff should gain experience and level up. As they become more skilled, they may expect better pay. If the player underpays them for too long, they may leave.

This should feel fair and human rather than punitive.

The staff system should support the retail loop. It should not become a separate management game.

---

# 5. What The Game Is

The Garden Stall is:

* a cosy mobile retail sim
* a stock-buying and selling game
* a location-based demand puzzle
* a plant recommendation game
* a display and merchandising game
* a light seasonal planning game
* a gentle reputation-building game
* a small-business progression game
* a game about living inventory
* a game where plant knowledge is useful but learnable

The player experience should centre on:

* browsing wholesale stock
* planning tomorrow’s trade
* arranging the stall
* watching customers browse
* making occasional active recommendations
* managing light care pressure
* improving the van/stall
* growing reputation
* eventually hiring and retaining staff

---

# 6. What The Game Is Not

The Garden Stall is not:

* a full garden centre management game
* a realistic nursery simulator
* a propagation game
* a farm sim
* a botanical science simulator
* a greenhouse automation sim
* a hard economy game
* a logistics simulator
* a stressful HR management game
* a disease and pest management game
* a detailed plant-care simulator

The game should avoid systems that pull attention away from the core experience of buying, displaying, selling, recommending, and lightly maintaining stock.

The project should be especially careful not to drift into:

* seed sowing and propagation as a major system
* detailed soil/nutrient management
* exact watering simulation
* realistic pest control
* complex supply chains
* heavy staff scheduling
* large fixed-premises business management
* spreadsheet-heavy accounting
* full open-town life sim mechanics

These may sound thematically related, but they are not central to this game.

---

# 7. Target Player Experience

A good session of The Garden Stall should feel like this:

The player checks tomorrow’s possible locations. The village green looks promising because the weather is sunny and local gardeners are expected. They browse the wholesaler website and notice lavender, salvia, herbs, and a cheap batch of slightly tired petunias. They buy carefully, knowing display space is limited.

Before opening, they arrange the stall. Lavender and salvia go near the front for colour and scent. Herbs go in a wooden crate. The petunias go on the side table, priced low. They leave a small reduced basket ready.

During the day, most customers browse. Some buy automatically. A few walk away, giving useful feedback: they wanted shade plants, but the player brought mostly sun-lovers. One customer asks for something bee-friendly for a pot by their mum’s back door. The player suggests a suitable combination and earns a strong review.

By closing time, the display is a little messy. Some herbs need water. The petunias are nearly past their best. The player reduces them, tidies the trays, checks reviews, and starts thinking about tomorrow.

The experience should create a pleasant loop of:

> Plan, stock, display, sell, learn, tidy, improve.

---

# 8. Approach to Plant Knowledge

The game should respect real plant knowledge but not demand expertise from the player at the start.

The early game should use:

* familiar plants
* clear tags
* obvious customer needs
* forgiving stock
* readable wholesaler notes
* helpful feedback
* simple displays

The game should teach through repetition and consequence.

For example:

A customer looking for a dark hallway plant should not require the player to already know every shade-tolerant houseplant. The plant cards, tags, and customer language should allow them to reason it out.

As the game progresses, requests may become more subtle:

* matching colour schemes
* choosing plants with compatible care needs
* identifying plants from lay descriptions
* avoiding toxic plants for pets
* selecting for seasonal continuity
* choosing plants for difficult conditions

Possible difficulty modes may adjust how much help the player receives, how quickly plants degrade, and how demanding customers are.

---

# 9. Approach to Realism

The Garden Stall should be grounded rather than fully realistic.

It should feel believable to someone with garden centre or plant retail experience, but it should simplify real-world complexity in favour of playability.

Realism should be used where it creates good decisions:

* plants have seasons
* plants can wilt
* display matters
* customers ask vague questions
* some plants are easier to sell while in flower
* reduced sections are commercially useful
* staff become valuable when the workload grows
* location affects demand

Realism should be avoided where it creates busywork:

* exact watering volume
* soil pH
* nutrient schedules
* pest diagnosis
* propagation timing
* licensing
* supplier paperwork
* van insurance
* payroll administration
* detailed accounting

The rule is:

> Use realism when it creates readable retail decisions. Cut realism when it creates maintenance chores.

---

# 10. Business and Economy Tone

The economy should matter, but the game should not be harsh.

The player should care about:

* margin
* cashflow
* overbuying
* stock going past peak
* stall fees
* pricing too high or too low
* upgrade costs
* staff wages
* reputation

But failure should usually be recoverable.

The game should create mild pressure, not anxiety.

Bad decisions may lead to:

* unsold stock
* reduced profit
* tired displays
* lower reviews
* staff dissatisfaction
* slower progression

They should not usually lead to abrupt game-over states.

The business fantasy is small-scale, personal, and constructive.

---

# 11. Progression Fantasy

The player starts scrappy.

Early state:

* ugly transit van
* trestle table
* small stock
* limited money
* no staff
* basic locations
* simple wholesaler access
* little reputation

Over time, they gain:

* better furniture
* display upgrades
* decorations
* branded signage
* more storage
* improved vehicle options
* better wholesalers
* more locations
* more customer trust
* staff
* rare or specialist stock
* stronger local reputation

Progression should be visible and emotional.

The stall should gradually move from:

> “A table beside a van.”

to:

> “A recognisable little travelling plant shop with style, regulars, and a reputation.”

---

# 12. Visual and Interface Direction

The Garden Stall should be visually warm, readable, and stock-focused.

The visual identity should prioritise:

* plants as attractive objects
* clear display zones
* readable customer needs
* satisfying before/after improvement
* seasonal colour
* cosy retail clutter
* van/stall personality
* recognisable plant silhouettes and colours

The interface should make stock decisions easy.

Important UI areas:

* wholesaler website
* current stock list
* plant cards
* display arrangement screen
* location selection
* customer request panel
* daily sales report
* reputation/review screen
* staff panel
* upgrade catalogue

The wholesaler interface should deliberately feel like browsing a simplified trade stock website, not like an abstract item shop.

Plant listings should include practical retail cues:

* wholesale price
* suggested retail
* condition
* season
* care difficulty
* stock quantity
* tags
* supplier note

---

# 13. Narrative and World Tone

The Garden Stall does not need a heavy story to work.

Its narrative can emerge through:

* locations
* recurring customers
* reviews
* staff personalities
* seasonal events
* local reputation
* upgrade milestones
* special requests
* small bits of customer memory

The tone should be lightly human and grounded.

Customers may remember good advice. Staff may grow in confidence. A location may become more welcoming as the stall’s reputation grows. A customer may return to say a plant survived, flowered, or became a gift someone loved.

The best narrative moments should arise from the retail loop, not interrupt it.

Examples:

* “That fern you sold me is still alive.”
* “My mum loved the planter.”
* “I told my neighbour you knew what that blue pom-pom shrub was.”
* “You’re the plant van everyone’s talking about.”

---

# 14. Relationship to Tiny Bookshop

Tiny Bookshop is a useful benchmark, but The Garden Stall must stand on its own.

Useful benchmark lessons:

* keep the core loop small
* make stock curation pleasurable
* let location shape demand
* use recommendations as charm moments
* make the shop feel personal
* let atmosphere carry the experience
* avoid overcomplicating the business layer

Where The Garden Stall differs:

* stock is alive
* display is more mechanically central
* seasonality matters more
* stock condition matters
* perishability matters
* reduced stock matters
* staff pressure can emerge from maintenance
* plant suitability is practical as well as aesthetic

The Garden Stall should not try to compete by being bigger in every direction. It should compete by making its own central fantasy richer:

> “I sell living things, so timing, care, display, and suitability matter.”

The following boundaries should protect the project from uncontrolled expansion.

* mobile plant stall

* customer demand profiles

* staff levelling
* staff pay expectations
* returning customers
* seasonal events

# 16. Prototype Vision

The first playable prototype should prove the central loop, not the full game.

Prototype 0.1 should focus on:

* 7 trading days
* 3 locations
* 20-30 plants
* simplified wholesaler website
* stock buying
* display arrangement
* passive browsing sales
* special requests
* basic pricing
* watering/tidying/consolidation
* reduced section
* daily feedback
* reputation score

It should not include:

* staff
* full seasonal year
* complex weather
* advanced upgrades
* propagation
* pests/disease
* deep narrative
* large plant catalogue

The prototype succeeds if the player thinks:

> “I want to do another trading day because I can already see how to stock and display better tomorrow.”

---

# 17. Design Tests

Any proposed feature should pass at least one of these tests:

## Stock Test

Does this make stock choice more interesting?

## Selling Test

Does this help the player understand or influence sales?

## Display Test

Does this make the stall more satisfying to arrange, improve, or look at?

## Customer Test

Does this make customer needs more readable, varied, or memorable?

## Pressure Test

Does this add gentle useful pressure without turning into chores?

## Progression Test

Does this make the stall feel like it is growing from scrappy to beloved?

## Scope Test

Can this be implemented without turning the game into a different genre?

If a feature passes none of these tests, it probably does not belong.

# 18. Current One-Sentence Definition

**The Garden Stall** is a cosy mobile plant retail sim about buying seasonal wholesale stock, arranging attractive displays, setting prices, and matching living plants to customer needs while managing the gentle pressure of stock condition, seasonality, and stall growth.

---

# 19. Current Short Pitch

Start with an old van, a trestle table, and a few trays of plants. Choose where to trade, browse the wholesaler for seasonal stock, arrange your display, set your prices, and sell to customers looking for gifts, herbs, houseplants, pollinator flowers, shade plants, and bargains. Most customers browse, but some need your help directly: identify a half-remembered shrub, build a bee-friendly pot, or steer them away from the wrong plant for a dark hallway. Keep your stock healthy, your display full, and your reputation growing as your scruffy van becomes a beloved travelling garden stall.

---

# 20. Final Vision Lock

The Garden Stall should always return to this central idea:

> A small mobile plant business, built through good stock judgement, attractive display, customer understanding, and the gentle complications of selling living things.

If a proposed system strengthens that idea, it may belong.

If it pulls the game toward farming, nursery simulation, heavy business management, or generic life sim expansion, it should be cut or deferred.
