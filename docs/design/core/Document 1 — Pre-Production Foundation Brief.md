# THE GARDEN STALL

## Pre-Production Foundation Brief 001

**Working Title:** The Garden Stall
**Project Type:** Cosy mobile plant retail sim
**Current Status:** Concept definition / pre-production preparation
**Purpose of this Document:** Define what must be understood, specified, and protected before full content development begins.

---

# 1. Project Summary

**The Garden Stall** is a cosy mobile retail game about buying seasonal plant stock from a wholesaler, arranging an attractive stall display, setting prices, selling to customers, and managing the gentle pressure of living inventory.

The player begins with an ugly transit-style van, a trestle table, limited cash, and a small selection of wholesale plants. Over time, they improve the stall with furniture, displays, decorations, better vehicles, staff, and reputation.

The core fantasy is not “running a large garden centre” or “simulating horticulture in detail.” It is:

> Becoming the owner of a charming mobile garden stall, learning what plants people want, making beautiful displays, and matching living stock to the right customers, places, and seasons.

The game is strongly comparable to **Tiny Bookshop** in structure and mood: a cosy mobile retail game where stock choice, location, customer needs, and atmosphere matter. However, The Garden Stall’s key difference is that plants are living stock. They can wilt, pass their flowering peak, clutter displays, require light care, and become reduced stock if not sold in time.

---

# 2. Core Design Pillars

## 2.1 Stock and Sell, Not Plant Care

The Garden Stall is primarily a **stock-and-sell game**.

Plant care exists as a small side pressure, not as the main game. The player is not expected to micromanage soil chemistry, propagation, disease, pest control, nutrients, exact watering levels, or detailed botanical growth.

Care mechanics should serve four purposes:

1. Keep the player lightly busy between sales.
2. Create perishability and pressure around living stock.
3. Encourage better organisation and stock planning.
4. Justify staff hiring and automation as the business grows.

The player should feel like a stallholder with living stock, not a full-time nursery technician.

---

## 2.2 Location-Led Selling

The player chooses where to trade. Each location determines the likely customer profile, footfall, budgets, display expectations, and plant demand.

Locations are not just cosmetic backdrops. They are strategic selling environments.

Example location effects:

* A city square may favour office workers, flat-dwellers, gifts, and houseplants.
* A village green may favour bedding plants, cottage garden flowers, retirees, and pollinator plants.
* A farmers’ market may favour herbs, vegetable seedlings, practical buyers, and wildlife-friendly stock.
* A garden show may favour experienced gardeners, unusual varieties, larger budgets, and more demanding requests.

A good day begins before opening: the player should ask, “Where am I trading tomorrow, and what stock/display will suit that place?”

---

## 2.3 Customer Needs and Plant Matching

Customers arrive with needs. Most customers browse passively and may buy if the stall contains suitable plants at suitable prices in an attractive display.

Customer needs are defined by tags and context, such as:

* indoor plant
* shady garden
* full sun
* drought tolerant
* low maintenance
* gift
* scented
* pollinator friendly
* child-safe
* pet-safe
* colourful now
* suitable for pots
* good for beginners

The game should reward the player for understanding plant suitability without requiring expert knowledge at the beginning.

The player should learn plants through play.

---

## 2.4 Special Requests

Most customers browse passively, but approximately **one in ten** customers should create a special request.

Special requests are active recommendation challenges. These are the game’s clearest equivalent to Tiny Bookshop’s direct recommendation moments.

Special requests may include:

* identifying a plant from a layperson’s description
* choosing a plant for an awkward garden condition
* assembling a companion pot set
* recommending a gift
* helping a customer avoid a bad choice
* matching plants to a season, colour scheme, or emotional memory
* suggesting something the customer would not find by passive browsing

Examples:

> “My neighbour used to have this bush with big blue pom-pom flowers. Do you know what it was?”

> “I need something for a shady front step that won’t look miserable.”

> “Can you make me a cheerful bee-friendly pot for my mum?”

> “I want something pretty for my bathroom, but it does not get much light.”

Completing special requests should create rewards beyond the sale itself:

* reputation increase
* positive review
* returning customer
* word-of-mouth boost
* location profile increase
* unlock progress
* occasional story or relationship progression

Special requests are one of the game’s main sources of charm, expertise, and memorable moments.

---

## 2.5 Display and Merchandising

Display is a central feature of The Garden Stall.

The stall is not only storage. It is a selling surface, visual puzzle, and expression of the player’s taste.

Good display should improve browsing sales, increase impulse purchases, support higher prices, and make the stall feel loved.

Display quality may consider:

* empty tray slots
* healthy vs tired plants
* tidy vs cluttered presentation
* colour harmony
* seasonal appropriateness
* plant height and shape contrast
* use of furniture
* featured plants
* reduced section placement
* matching the display to the location

A stall full of healthy, well-arranged, seasonally appropriate stock should sell better than a messy table with gaps, wilting plants, and poor colour balance.

Display should be meaningful, but not fiddly. It should use clear zones, trays, shelves, crates, stands, and feature areas rather than demanding precise freeform placement.

---

## 2.6 Staff as Pressure Release and Progression

As the business grows, the player should eventually feel that doing everything alone is inefficient.

Staff can be hired to help with:

* watering
* tidying
* pruning/deadheading
* tray consolidation
* sales
* customer patience
* display maintenance
* restocking
* reduced-section management

Staff should learn, level up, and become more valuable over time.

A staff member may:

* become faster
* make fewer mistakes
* gain specialist traits
* improve customer satisfaction
* improve display quality
* handle certain request types better
* reduce daily workload

However, staff should also expect fair pay. If they level up but are not paid what they are worth, they may become unhappy or leave.

This should be framed gently, not harshly. A good staff member leaving should feel like a natural consequence of growth and ambition, not a punitive failure state.

# 3. What The Game Is

The Garden Stall is:

* a cosy mobile retail sim
* a stock selection game
* a plant recommendation game
* a display and merchandising game
* a light seasonal planning game
* a gentle staff progression game
* a game about matching plants to people and places
* a game where living inventory creates small pressure

The intended emotional tone is:

* warm
* busy but not frantic
* charming
* practical
* lightly educational
* visually satisfying
* seasonal
* grounded in real plant retail experience

The player should feel clever, not overwhelmed.

---

# 4. What The Game Is Not

The Garden Stall is not:

* a realistic nursery simulator
* a full garden centre management sim
* a farm game
* a propagation simulator
* a botanical science simulator
* a complex logistics game
* a hard business survival game
* a spreadsheet economy game
* a stressful staff management game
* a detailed plant disease or pest simulator

The project should avoid drifting into systems that do not directly support stock selection, selling, display, customer matching, light care pressure, and stall progression.

---

# 5. Benchmark: Tiny Bookshop

Tiny Bookshop is a useful benchmark because it demonstrates a strong cosy mobile retail loop:

* choose where to trade
* curate stock
* open the shop
* meet customers
* recommend suitable items
* earn money
* decorate and improve the shop
* build atmosphere and local identity

The Garden Stall can borrow from this structure, but should not simply copy it.

## Similarities

Both games involve:

* mobile retail
* location choice
* stock curation
* customer needs
* recommendation moments
* cosy tone
* gradual shop improvement
* charm over intense optimisation

## Key Difference

Tiny Bookshop sells books. Books do not wilt, flower, pass their peak, need watering, clash visually in displays, or become reduced stock because their best week has passed.

The Garden Stall sells living inventory.

This creates the game’s distinct identity:

> Living stock + seasonal demand + visual display + light care pressure.

# 6. Core Day Loop

A typical day should contain the following phases.

---

## 6.1 Planning / Evening Phase

The player prepares for the next trading day.

Possible actions:

* choose tomorrow’s location
* browse wholesaler website
* buy plant stock
* check current inventory
* set prices
* arrange display
* move plants to reduced section
* water/tidy/prune stock
* assign staff tasks
* review reputation, reviews, and customer trends

The player should be thinking:

> “Where am I going tomorrow, who will shop there, and what stock/display will suit them?”

---

## 6.2 Setup Phase

The stall is prepared at the chosen location.

Possible factors:

* available display space
* weather/exposure
* location fee
* footfall forecast
* customer mix
* display attractiveness
* staff assigned
* featured stock
* reduced section visibility

The game should give the player clear feedback on likely demand without solving the puzzle for them.

---

## 6.3 Trading Phase

Customers arrive.

Most customers browse passively. They may buy plants if:

* suitable stock is available
* prices are acceptable
* display is attractive
* plants are healthy/presentable
* location demand matches stock
* staff/player availability is sufficient

Some customers trigger special requests.

The player may:

* handle special recommendations
* adjust price or display during the day, if allowed
* water or tidy stock during quiet moments
* restock from van storage
* decide whether to discount declining plants
* observe browsing behaviour and missed opportunities

---

## 6.4 End-of-Day Phase

The trading day resolves.

The game reports:

* sales
* profit/loss
* missed demand
* customer satisfaction
* display feedback
* special request outcomes
* stock condition changes
* reputation/review movement
* staff experience
* plants that passed peak or need reduction
* plants that degraded or died

The player then prepares for the next day.

## 7.2 Controlled Tag Vocabulary

A controlled tag list must be created before large-scale plant content is written.

This is essential.

If plant writers invent tags freely, the matching system will become inconsistent and unusable.

Example problem:

* “bee friendly”
* “pollinator”
* “pollinator-friendly”
* “wildlife”
* “good for bees”
* “nectar plant”

All of those might mean similar things, but the game needs one standard tag, such as:

* pollinator-friendly

The tag bible should define:

* allowed tags
* tag meaning
* when to use each tag
* incompatible tags
* example plants for each tag

# 8. Wholesaler System

The wholesaler experience should feel like browsing a simplified trade website.

This is the main stock-buying interface.

The wholesaler should provide enough information for the player to make meaningful commercial decisions without overwhelming them.

---

## 8.1 Wholesaler Listing Example

**Tray of 6 Lavender ‘Hidcote’**

* Wholesale price: £12
* Suggested retail: £4 each
* Expected margin: good
* Season: strong summer seller
* Condition: buds just colouring
* Care: low
* Tags: outdoor, full sun, drought tolerant, scented, pollinator-friendly, cottage style
* Display notes: strong scent, good colour, neat shape
* Supplier note: “Compact batch, good for front-table summer displays.”

This gives the player useful information:

* what it costs
* what it might sell for
* who might want it
* how risky it is
* where it might display well
* how urgent the selling window is

---

## 8.2 Wholesaler Variables

Wholesale stock can vary by:

* season

* rarity
* supplier reliability
* current trend
* upcoming local events

* “Slightly leggy, but cheap.”
* “Excellent colour this week.”
* “Past peak soon — priced to clear.”
* “Limited batch.”
* “Popular with gift buyers.”
* “Good for dry weather forecast.”

The wholesaler should sometimes tempt the player with risky bargains.

# 9. Pricing and Reduced Stock

The player should set prices.

Pricing should be simple, readable, and meaningful.

Each plant should have:

* wholesale cost
* suggested price
* customer price tolerance
* premium tolerance
* discount threshold
* reduced-section value

Possible pricing choices:

* bargain price
* normal price
* premium price
* reduced price

High prices should reduce conversion unless supported by:

* wealthy location
* rare plant
* strong display
* excellent condition
* high demand
* special request suitability
* good reputation

Reduced stock should be a core part of the loop.

Plants may be moved to reduced when:

* past flowering peak
* slightly tired
* awkward leftover quantity
* out of season
* display no longer benefits from them
* player needs space

Reduced plants sell for less but help recover value, clear space, and attract bargain hunters.

The reduced section should be visually and commercially useful, but too much reduced stock should make the stall look tired.

## 10.1 Display Quality Factors

Display scoring may consider:

* no empty slots
* healthy plants
* tidy arrangement
* colour harmony
* seasonal relevance
* height variation
* matching furniture and plants
* feature plant clarity
* reduced section containment
* location suitability

Examples:

A lavender, salvia, nepeta, and scabious display in summer at a village green could score well for:

* colour harmony
* pollinator theme
* seasonal appropriateness
* cottage/wild style
* scent
* customer-location fit

A mixed table with empty slots, wilting basil, out-of-flower bulbs, and random colours may reduce browsing conversion.

---

## 10.2 Furniture and Display Items

Furniture should have mechanical qualities.

Examples:

**Trestle Table**

* Basic display surface
* Cheap
* Low charm
* Moderate capacity

**Wooden Crates**

* Rustic charm
* Good for herbs and cottage plants
* Improves market/village display appeal

**Tiered Shelves**

* Improves visibility of small pots
* Good for houseplants, herbs, and compact flowering plants

**Hanging Rail**

* Allows hanging baskets/trailing plants
* Good visual draw
* Strong for summer locations

**Shaded Awning**

* Slows degradation for shade-loving and indoor plants
* Improves comfort and browsing time

**Chalkboard Sign**

* Allows featured category
* Boosts sales of promoted plant type

**Reduced Basket**

* Contains reduced stock neatly
* Prevents reduced stock from damaging main display too much

# 11. Customer System Requirements

Customers should be built from reusable archetypes.

Each archetype should define:

* typical locations
* budget range
* price sensitivity
* common needs
* preferred tags
* disliked tags
* patience
* impulse-buy tendency
* likelihood of special request
* review influence

---

## 11.1 Passive Browsing Customers

Most customers should browse and buy without direct player interaction.

A passive customer should evaluate:

* available stock
* plant suitability
* price
* display quality
* plant condition
* location fit
* reputation
* staff support

The player should receive feedback when customers do not buy.

Examples:

* “Several people looked for herbs today, but you had none.”
* “The peace lilies attracted attention, but the price was too high for this location.”
* “Your lavender display drew people in from the street.”
* “The reduced section sold well, but made the stall feel a little tired.”
* “Two customers wanted shade plants; the hostas sold quickly.”

This feedback is essential because passive systems must be legible.

---

## 11.2 Special Request Customers

Special request customers create authored or semi-authored challenges.

Possible categories:

### Identification Requests

The customer describes a plant informally.

Example:

> “It had huge round blue flowers, like pom-poms. My neighbour had one by the wall.”

Likely answer: hydrangea.

### Suitability Requests

The customer has a specific condition.

Example:

> “I have a dry sunny balcony and I always forget to water.”

Good answers: lavender, rosemary, thyme, sedum, aloe, depending on indoor/outdoor context.

### Companion Pot Requests

The customer wants a combination.

Example:

> “Can you make a pot that looks good together and brings bees in?”

Good answer: compatible sun-loving pollinator plants with colour harmony and similar water needs.

### Gift Requests

The customer needs something for someone else.

Example:

> “I need something cheerful for my aunt. She likes colour but not fuss.”

Good answers: flowering, low-maintenance, attractive-now plants.

### Correction Requests

The customer wants something unsuitable.

Example:

> “I want lavender for a dark bathroom.”

The player should be rewarded for redirecting them gently to a better choice.

---

## 11.3 Special Request Rewards

Success may grant:

* sale value
* bonus tip
* positive review
* returning customer
* reputation increase
* shop profile boost
* unlock progress
* local rumour/word-of-mouth
* staff experience, if staff helped

Failure should be gentle:

* no sale
* minor reputation loss
* neutral customer response
* lesson feedback
* possible partial success

# 15. Difficulty and Plant Knowledge

The game should support low-bar entry.

At the start, the player should not need expert plant knowledge.

Early game should feature:

* familiar plants
* clear tags
* simple customer needs
* forgiving stock
* readable wholesaler notes
* gentle feedback
* obvious display improvements

Later difficulty may increase through:

* specialist locations
* more subtle plant requests
* higher customer expectations
* seasonal risk
* fussy plants
* rare stock
* more complex companion planting
* higher price sensitivity
* staff management pressure

Possible difficulty modes or progression levels:

## Relaxed

* clearer hints
* slower degradation
* forgiving customers
* stronger tag visibility
* lower staff pressure

## Standard

* balanced demand, care, and pricing

## Expert / Horticultural

* fewer hints
* more plant knowledge required
* sharper seasonality
* more demanding special requests
* stronger consequences for poor stock choices

The game should teach first, then test.

# 19. Open Questions

The following questions should be resolved before or during the next specification stage.

## Core Identity

# 20. Primary Production Risks

## Risk 1: Plant Tag Sprawl

If tags are not controlled early, plant data becomes inconsistent and unusable.

Mitigation:

* Create Plant Schema and Tag Bible before generating plant content.

---

## Risk 2: Feature Drift Into Nursery Simulation

Because plants are living things, the design may drift into propagation, disease, compost, pests, genetics, irrigation, greenhouse management, and other large systems.

Mitigation:

* Maintain the principle: stock and sell, not plant care.

---

## Risk 3: Display Becomes Fiddly

Display is central, but if placement is too detailed it may become slow and annoying.

Mitigation:

* Use zones, trays, shelves, and slots rather than free placement.

---

## Risk 4: Passive Sales Feel Opaque

If customers browse automatically, players may not understand why sales succeed or fail.

Mitigation:

* Provide clear daily feedback, customer comments, and visible demand signals.

---

## Risk 5: Special Requests Become Too Hard

If plant knowledge demands are too high, players may feel excluded.

Mitigation:

* Start with familiar plants and clear hints.
* Increase difficulty gradually.
* Allow plant cards and filtering to support learning.

---

## Risk 6: Staff Becomes a Separate Management Game

Staff learning and pay expectations are good, but could dominate the game if overbuilt.

Mitigation:

* Keep staff focused on supporting stock, display, care, and sales.

---

## Risk 7: Wholesaler Interface Feels Dry

Stock ordering could become a boring spreadsheet.

Mitigation:

* Present it as a simplified trade website with useful notes, seasonal cues, batch condition, and tempting bargains.

---

## Risk 8: Too Much Content Before Systems Are Stable

Creating hundreds of plants before the schema is stable would waste work.

Mitigation:

* Build Prototype 0.1 with 20–30 plants first.
* Expand only after the core matching/display/economy loop works.

# 22. Current Working Definition

**The Garden Stall** is a cosy mobile plant retail sim where the player buys seasonal wholesale plants, arranges attractive displays, sets prices, sells to location-specific customers, handles occasional plant-matching requests, and manages the light pressure of living stock. The game is about stock judgement, display, customer suitability, and stall growth — not detailed plant care.

Its key identity is:

> Tiny Bookshop, but with living stock, seasonal timing, visual merchandising, and the gentle chaos of plants that need to be sold before they stop looking their best.
